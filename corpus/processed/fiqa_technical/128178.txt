It's slightly more insidious than that. These days we're finding out how to make games in such a way as to maximise profit. Not by making the games more fun or entertaining but by abusing the flaws in human psychology. They are also designed in such a way as to incentivise these extra purchases. When it says in the article you can buy quicker progress through the game and a lot of people like it, it's because they've artificially slowed down the normal progress in the game. They've deliberately broken the product so you'll pay to fix it. From a business ethics point of view there's nothing wrong in this but it makes a lot of people in the industry uneasy.
